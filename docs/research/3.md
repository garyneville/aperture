1. Satellite-derived cloud observation The provided research does not explicitly identify a free API that returns a direct "observed cloud cover percentage" point value with low latency. However, it does highlight the Open-Meteo Satellite Radiation API as a highly relevant proxy. This API provides real-time, backward-averaged solar radiation data derived from satellite observations, including the EUMETSAT MSG (15-minute resolution, 2-hour delay) and the new EUMETSAT MTG (10-minute resolution, 20-to-30-minute delay). You can use this to infer cloud clearing by comparing the API's shortwave_radiation (which accounts for actual clouds) against shortwave_radiation_clear_sky (which excludes clouds) to flag when the atmosphere is clearing faster than NWP models predict.
12. Sunset colour physics Your AOD range of 0.08–0.18 is slightly too narrow. The research identifies the ideal "Goldilocks Zone" for vibrant sunsets as an AOD between 0.1 and 0.25. Below 0.05, the sky is too exceptionally clear to scatter sprawling colour, and above 0.4, severe attenuation washes out contrast.
Humidity is a massive physical modifier that you must include. You should target a relative humidity of 30% to 60%. When humidity exceeds 70%, hygroscopic aerosols swell with water vapour, creating a milky haze that absorbs light and ruins colour vibrancy. For a derived feature, you can adapt the empirical formula for Crepuscular Ray Intensity (CRI) developed by Van Den Broeke: CRI=−0.5279−0.1552(WindSpeed)−0.0373(Rain)+0.0629(Dewpoint)+0.2741(CloudType). To optimize for vivid color, your formula should triage in this order: Cloud cover 30–70%, AOD 0.1–0.25, and RH 30–60%.

Real-time phenology The research identifies the Pan-European Phenological Database (PEP725), maintained by GeoSphere Austria, as a primary source for European-wide phenological observations with open, unrestricted access for science and education. If you prefer to compute this dynamically from your existing weather data, the research validates using a temperature-accumulation model (Growing Degree Days) by summing the daily accumulated temperature above a baseline of 5°C to track seasonal progress.
14. Golden-hour cloud sweet spot The research strongly supports your bell curve centred at 50%. The optimal cloud coverage for dramatic sunsets is explicitly cited as between 30% and 70%, with the sweet spot peaking perfectly at 50%.
However, the best sunset photos absolutely correlate more with cloud altitude distribution than total percentage. High clouds (cirrus, >6,000m) and mid-level clouds (altocumulus, 2,000–7,000m) are ideal because they sit high enough in the troposphere to catch and reflect reddened light up to 30 minutes after the sun has set at the surface. Conversely, low clouds (<2,000m) are detrimental as they simply block the light path and cause grey washouts.
15. Astro moon-washout model The research confirms that the Krisciunas-Schaefer model evaluates moonlight brightness based on phase, geometry, and extinction, but the sources do not provide the exact mathematical parameters or extinction factors for the K-S model.
Your 25% threshold for "moonlight starts mattering", however, is highly accurate. A proposed "Moon washout severity table" demonstrates that for broadband deep-sky imaging, a moon phase of 15% to 30% introduces a "Low penalty," while anything above 30% triggers "High" to "Fatal" penalties. The research heavily emphasises that you must combine this phase percentage with moon altitude, as a bright but low moon has significantly less washout impact than a high moon due to increased atmospheric extinction.

Mist formation thresholds The research states that radiation fog relies on "near-saturation" and a "small dew-point spread in the lowest 1km," with the dew-point deficit being the strongest single predictor. However, the sources do not specify whether a ≤ 2.5°C or ≤ 1.5°C gate is empirically superior for scoring.
Regarding your Boundary Layer Height (BLH) cutoff, the sources note that "valley fog that stays photogenic" is driven by cold air pooling and terrain drainage flows. The provided texts do not supply a specific numerical BLH cutoff in meters to distinguish between lifting and pooling fog. Instead, they advise combining high-resolution terrain downscaling (e.g., 90-meter DEM) with the Fog Stability Index (FSI < 31) to identify where cold air and moisture are actively being trapped in valleys.
